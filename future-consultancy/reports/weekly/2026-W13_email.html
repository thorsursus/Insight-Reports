Agentic AI Enters the Consulting Workforce at Scale

Strategy OpEx

The traditional consulting staffing pyramid — partners at the top, masses of junior analysts at the base — is being fundamentally reshaped. McKinsey now deploys roughly 25,000 AI agents alongside its ~40,000 human consultants, while Accenture has committed $3 billion and plans to double its AI workforce from 40,000 to 80,000. These aren't experimental chatbots; they're autonomous workflow participants handling analytical tasks that previously required junior team members.

PA Consulting's research on Nordic companies identifies four distinct AI adoption patterns: general task assistants, knowledge agents trained on company data, decision support systems, and autonomous AI agents. Most Nordic firms remain at levels 1–2. The jump to levels 3–4 — where real competitive advantage lies — is precisely where consulting value is highest.

Nordic Regulation Converges — Compliance as Opportunity

BizTransform Nordic

Nordic organizations now face four overlapping regulatory frameworks arriving simultaneously: the EU AI Act, NIS2, GDPR, and ISO 42001. Analysis shows nearly half of the requirements across these frameworks overlap — yet most organizations still approach them as separate compliance workstreams. This fragmented approach creates redundant effort and audit fatigue, opening a clear lane for consulting firms that can offer integrated regulatory frameworks.

The timelines differ meaningfully across the region: Denmark has had full NIS2 compliance mandatory since July 2025, giving Danish experience a head start. Sweden's NIS2 Act entered force in January 2026. Norway targets summer 2026 for AI Act implementation. These staggered timelines mean cross-border lessons transfer — a firm advising across the Nordics has a structural advantage.

The Outcome Economy: Consulting's Business Model Shift

Strategy OpEx

Capgemini's strategy chief publicly stated this week that the consulting model is shifting from selling technology and advice to selling outcomes. This echoes what MIT Sloan's Davenport and Bean flagged as a key 2026 trend: generative AI becoming an organizational resource rather than an individual tool. When AI is organizational, consulting must deliver organization-level outcomes — not person-hours.

Meanwhile, Davenport and Bean also rank AI bubble deflation as their #1 trend for 2026, warning of economic hits as overhyped valuations correct. This creates a paradox: clients need pragmatic AI guidance more than ever, but budgets may tighten. Firms positioned as "outcome partners" rather than "AI evangelists" will weather this correction best.

Implication for Knowit: Time to evaluate which engagements could be repriced around outcomes rather than hours. The Operational Excellence practice could lead internal transformation toward outcome-based delivery. Position as "pragmatic AI" to survive potential budget scrutiny.
